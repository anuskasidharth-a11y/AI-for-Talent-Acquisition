# Recruiting AI Best Practices

## When to Use AI

Drafting job descriptions, structuring boolean strings, summarizing long documents, and writing first drafts of candidate communication. Anywhere the task is producing a starting point, not a final decision.

## When Not to Use AI

Final hiring decisions, compensation decisions that will be communicated to a candidate, and any assessment of a candidate's character or interview performance. These require human judgment because they carry consequences for a real person's livelihood, and AI has no accountability for getting them wrong.

## Candidate Privacy

I do not paste full resumes, contact details, or client-confidential role information into public AI tools without checking whether the platform trains on submitted data. For anything involving personal identifiable information, I strip names and contact details before using AI to summarize or analyze, and I add them back manually afterward.

## Bias Awareness

AI models can replicate patterns in their training data, including biased ones, like favoring resumes with certain school names or penalizing employment gaps. I do not let AI screen candidates in or out on its own. I use it to help organize information, and I make the actual judgment call myself, specifically watching for whether an AI-generated summary is quietly filtering out nontraditional backgrounds.

## Fact Checking

Anything AI generates about a candidate's claims, a company's background, or a market compensation figure gets verified before it reaches a client or a candidate. I treat AI output as a first draft of a fact, not a confirmed fact.

## Human Review

Every piece of AI-assisted writing that goes external, whether that is a rejection message or a client update, gets read by me in full before it is sent. Not skimmed. Read.

## Data Security

I do not use AI tools that store submitted data indefinitely for anything containing client-confidential deal terms or candidate personal data. When in doubt, I check the tool's data retention policy before pasting anything sensitive.

## Cost and Time Tradeoffs

AI saves real time on drafting, but the verification step it requires is not free. In practice, I have found the net time saved is meaningful but smaller than the raw drafting speed suggests. A job description that took 45 minutes to write now takes 15 minutes to draft and 10 minutes to verify and edit, which is still a real gain, but not the 90 percent time reduction it might look like at first glance.

## Ethical Considerations

Candidates deserve to know when they are interacting with AI-generated content versus a human recruiter, especially in outreach. I do not misrepresent AI-written messages as fully personal notes when they are templated, even if AI helped me personalize them.

## Final Recruiter Responsibility

AI does not carry accountability. I do. If an AI-assisted message goes out with a factual error, a biased filter, or a tone that damages a candidate relationship, that is on me, not the tool. Every best practice in this file exists because the responsibility never transfers.
