# AI Limitations and Human Checkpoints

## Why AI Isn't Enough

AI is fast and pattern-based. Recruiting decisions involve real people's careers and real client relationships. Those two things do not automatically align, so I treat every AI output as a draft that needs a human checkpoint before it affects anyone.

## Hallucinations

AI can invent a certification, a company name, or a statistic that sounds plausible but is not real. I once had a draft summary state that a candidate had "led a team of 12," a detail that was not anywhere in the source resume. The model had inferred it from context clues in the job title. I caught it because I read the source resume side by side with the summary, which is now a standing rule for me.

## Missing Context

AI does not know that a candidate's employment gap was a medical leave I discussed with them on a call, or that a client specifically deprioritized a skill listed in the original job requisition. It only knows what is in the prompt or document I gave it. If I do not include that context, it will generate a summary or a message that misses something a human recruiter would obviously account for.

## False Positives

AI screening tools can rate a candidate highly based on keyword density rather than actual fit, surfacing someone who lists the right skills but has no real depth in them.

## False Negatives

The reverse happens just as often. A strong candidate gets filtered out because their resume uses different terminology than the job description, even though their experience matches closely.

## Bias

Training data can encode patterns that quietly disadvantage nontraditional career paths, candidates from certain regions, or resumes with career gaps. I do not let AI make an inclusion or exclusion decision without my own review of the same candidate.

## Human Validation Checklist

**Before sourcing**
- Confirm the boolean string reflects the actual must-haves from the intake call, not just the job description as written
- Check that AI-suggested search terms are not narrowing the pool in a way that excludes qualified nontraditional candidates

**Before screening**
- Read the original resume myself, not just an AI summary of it
- Verify any specific claim, number, or credential the AI flagged as notable

**Before outreach**
- Confirm the message does not state or imply a compensation figure I did not provide
- Check the tone matches how I would actually speak to this person

**Before presenting candidates**
- Re-verify any fact from an AI-assisted summary against the original source document
- Confirm I am presenting my own judgment on fit, not an AI-generated fit score, as the basis for the recommendation

This checklist exists because every step above is a place where a hallucination, a missing piece of context, or a quiet bias can slip through if I let AI output move forward unchecked. Responsible use of AI in this work means being the last checkpoint before it affects a real person or a real client relationship.
